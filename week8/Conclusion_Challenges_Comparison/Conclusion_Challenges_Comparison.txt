Conclusion and Final Observations:

This laboratory exercise successfully bridged the gap between environment mapping and autonomous movement. By deploying the Nav2 stack on the TurtleBot3 within the Gazebo simulation, we executed a complete robotic navigation pipeline. The experience highlighted how an autonomous system leverages a static occupancy grid generated in Lab 5 as the essential roadmap for the global planner in Lab 7. We successfully utilized the FollowWaypoints action server to automate a complex sequence of five waypoints, proving that robotic missions can be executed autonomously with minimal manual intervention once the environment is known.  One of the most prominent difficulties encountered during the lab was ensuring accurate initial localization. The Adaptive Monte Carlo Localization (AMCL) system proved highly sensitive to its starting state; if the initial 2D pose estimate was poor, the simulated LiDAR scans misaligned with the mapped walls, leading to navigation failures and requiring manual recalibration via teleoperation. Another challenge was managing dynamic, unmapped obstacles that suddenly blocked the planned route. Fortunately, the system demonstrated strong resilience through the local costmap and the behavior tree recovery system, effectively avoiding collisions by backing up, waiting, and recalculating entirely new global paths around the obstacles.  

Workflow Comparison: SLAM (Lab 5) versus Autonomous Navigation (Lab 7)

The workflows of Lab 5 and Lab 7 represent two distinct but deeply connected phases of robotics. In the Lab 5 SLAM pipeline, the primary objective was to explore a completely unknown environment to generate a reliable floor plan. This required the Cartographer algorithm to simultaneously localize the robot and incrementally build the map from scratch. Because the system had no prior environmental knowledge, the robot relied entirely on manual teleoperation to ensure complete sensor coverage, with the primary challenge being the successful closure of mapping loops. The final output of this manual exploration phase was the saved static map files.  Conversely, the Lab 7 navigation pipeline focused on moving efficiently and safely through that now-known environment. Instead of building a map, it utilized the pre-loaded static map from Lab 5 alongside AMCL for localization and Nav2 for global and local path planning. The control method shifted entirely from manual driving to fully autonomous operation. The core challenges evolved from map creation to setting an accurate initial pose and reacting dynamically to newly introduced obstacles. Ultimately, SLAM represents the manual, computationally expensive process of learning a space, while Navigation represents the fully autonomous execution phase that leverages that pre-built data.
